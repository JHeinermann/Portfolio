A Year in Madagascar

I didn't want to go straight to university after school and wanted to spend a gap year abroad. Madagascar was the perfect country: completely different nature, completely different culture and above all, lots of geckos! I had already kept Madagascan reptiles in a terrarium and had met a professor from Braunschweig at a conference who told me that I could spend a year there studying the behaviour of frogs.

So, naive as I was, I set off for Madagascar. When I arrived in the small town of Andasibe, I was able to stay with a Malagasy family and learn about their rich culture. Among other things, I learnt how to cook the perfect rice and that a lot more people can fit in a car than you might think. I asked lots of questions to better understand the culture and even learned to speak a little Malagasy.

Madagascar's biodiversity left me speechless as I found more geckos, chameleons, snakes and frogs than I could count during my stay. I'm grateful to have had the opportunity to go on this trip and meet so many amazing people!

Studying Forest Sciences

During my stay in Madagascar, I realized that I no longer wanted to study biology because I missed the human side of it. Instead, I decided to study forest sciences at the TU Dresden, which has the second oldest department of forest sciences in the world. Forest sciences combine biology with economics, society and sustainability, taking a more holistic view of natural ressources.

This diverse degree programme includes subjects such as silviculture, forest economics, history, inventory, biodiversity, zoology, botany and statistics. I gained the ability to think long-term, beyond decades, and to consider multiple perspectives, from conservation to hunting, recreation, and economics.

Driven by a curiosity for new scientific methods, I was particularly drawn to integrated pest management. This fascinating approach minimizes the use of pesticides, by using natural antagonists. My fascination was expressed in my bachelor thesis, where I examined statistical models for evaluating new bark beetle traps.

During my masters, I worked in the award-winning "Biowild" Project in which I used my R-skills to improve data cleansing and to automate statistical analysis and visualization. While helping friends and fellow students with programming and statistics of their thesis, I recognized that many students lack some basic R-knowledge. I therefore designed a course titled "R - programming and statistics for your thesis".

For my master thesis, I delved deeper into modelling and explored the influence of poplar leaf beetles on poplar growth. I wanted to know under which circumstances, insecticide application could be economically justified. The model was planned as a tool to help farmers decide whether to use pesticide or not instead of a preventive application of pesticides.

The model I used is an 'individual-based model', which simulates single individuals and focuses on interactions between individuals and between individuals and their environment. I wanted to learn more about these models and decided to start a PhD.

My Modelling PhD

The pine forests of north-eastern Germany are dominated by a single tree species. This homogeneity reduces biodiversity and poses a significant risk. A single fungus, extreme weather event or insect infestation could devastate the entire ecosystem. Foresters are therefore converting pure stands into diverse mixed forests. However, predicting the growth and interactions within these multi-species communities is a unique challenge.

Unlike traditional models that treat forests as a single unit, individual-based models allow us to simulate the growth and interactions of individual trees within a forest, providing a detailed picture of the dynamic ecosystem. I am developing an individual-based model that focuses on the regeneration of young forests. Although the early years of a forest are crucial for predicting long-term forest composition and growth, they are difficult to describe and have not yet been simulated in detail in forest growth models.

Forest dynamics are driven by competition between trees for resources, especially light and water, so water and light availability and it's effects on young tree growth are key components of my model. Light availability is calculated for each tree within the canopy, taking into account complex interactions between neighbouring trees of different species and sizes. Water competition, especially during the scarce summer months, is also modelled in great detail, revealing the dynamic struggle for survival.

By incorporating different climate change scenarios into our models, we can predict the potential impact of drought and management interventions on tree growth and species composition, allowing us to proactively adapt forest management strategies. This foresight enables us to build resilient forests that can thrive in the face of a changing climate.
